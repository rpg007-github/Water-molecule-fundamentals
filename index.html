01Water's Molecular Structure

Water is H2O: two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to one oxygen atom. To understand water's shape, you need to understand what is happening with the electrons on oxygen.

Oxygen is in Group 6A (16) of the periodic table, which means it has six valence electrons (the electrons in its outermost shell that are available for bonding). In a covalent bond, two atoms share a pair of electrons. Oxygen shares one of its valence electrons with each hydrogen, forming two O-H covalent bonds. That uses up two of oxygen's six valence electrons in bonding. The remaining four valence electrons are not shared with anything. They sit on the oxygen atom in two pairs. These are called lone pairs (sometimes called "nonbonding pairs").

What Is a Lone Pair?
A lone pair is a pair of valence electrons on an atom that is not involved in any bond. They belong entirely to that one atom. You cannot see them, but they occupy space and exert repulsion just like bonding electrons do. On oxygen in water, there are two lone pairs. They are critical because they affect the molecule's shape, its polarity, and its ability to form hydrogen bonds.

So around the oxygen atom, there are four groups of electrons: two bonding pairs (the O-H bonds) and two lone pairs. According to VSEPR theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion), electron groups repel each other and spread out as far apart as possible. Four groups arrange themselves in a tetrahedral pattern in three-dimensional space, which is the electron geometry of water.

However, molecular geometry describes only where the atoms are, not where the lone pairs are. Since only two of the four electron groups are bonds to hydrogen atoms, the molecular geometry of water is bent (or V-shaped), with a bond angle of approximately 104.5 degrees. Why is this angle less than the ideal tetrahedral angle of 109.5 degrees? Because lone pairs are "fatter" than bonding pairs. A bonding pair is pulled between two nuclei and squeezed into a relatively narrow space. A lone pair belongs to only one atom and spreads out more broadly. The two lone pairs on oxygen push the two O-H bonding pairs closer together, compressing the angle from 109.5 degrees down to 104.5 degrees.

Key Point
Water's bent shape is the reason it is polar. If water were linear (like CO2), the two O-H bond dipoles would cancel and the molecule would be nonpolar. The bent geometry means the dipoles do not cancel, and the molecule has a permanent dipole moment. Every unique property of water traces back to this shape.
Common Mistake
Students often say water's electron geometry is "bent." It is not. The electron geometry is tetrahedral (four groups of electrons around oxygen). The molecular geometry is bent (only two atoms bonded to the central atom). Electron geometry and molecular geometry are different things.

02Polarity & Electronegativity

In a covalent bond, two atoms share electrons. But in most bonds, the sharing is not equal. One atom usually pulls the shared electrons closer to itself. Electronegativity is a number that describes how strongly an atom attracts shared electrons in a covalent bond. The higher the number, the stronger the pull.

Where Do Electronegativity Numbers Come From?

Electronegativity values are not something you measure directly with an instrument. They were developed by the chemist Linus Pauling in 1932 using bond energy data. Pauling noticed that bonds between different atoms (like O-H) were stronger than you would predict from the average of the same-atom bonds (O-O and H-H). He attributed the extra strength to the unequal sharing of electrons and used the size of that extra strength to assign a relative number to each element. This became the Pauling scale, which runs from about 0.7 (francium, weakest pull) to 4.0 (fluorine, strongest pull). These values appear in your textbook and on periodic table reference charts, and you are expected to use them, not memorize every one. The key ones for biology are: F = 4.0, O = 3.44, N = 3.04, C = 2.55, H = 2.20.

Common Mistake
Students sometimes treat electronegativity values as absolute physical measurements. They are not. They are a relative scale, meaning the numbers only have meaning when compared to each other. What matters is the difference in electronegativity between two bonded atoms, not the individual numbers by themselves.

Oxygen's electronegativity is 3.44. Hydrogen's is 2.20. The difference (ΔEN) is 1.24. This tells you the O-H bond is polar covalent: the electrons are shared, but unequally. Oxygen pulls the shared electrons closer, giving oxygen a partial negative charge (δ−) and each hydrogen a partial positive charge (δ+). The δ symbol (lowercase Greek delta) means "partial," meaning less than a full charge of +1 or −1. The atoms are not ions. They just have a slight imbalance of electron density.

This unequal sharing makes each O-H bond a polar covalent bond. Because the molecule is bent (Section 01), the two bond dipoles point in roughly the same direction rather than canceling out. The result is a molecule with a distinct positive end (the hydrogen side) and a negative end (the oxygen side). Water is a polar molecule.

Electronegativity Difference & Bond Type (General Rules)
ΔEN = 0: nonpolar covalent (equal sharing) | ΔEN = 0.1–1.7: polar covalent (unequal sharing) | ΔEN > 1.7: ionic (electron transferred)
O-H bond: ΔEN = 3.44 − 2.20 = 1.24 → polar covalent. These cutoffs are approximate guidelines, not rigid boundaries.
Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
Electronegativity and polarity are taught in general chemistry when you learn about bonding. In biology, this same concept explains why water is a good solvent, why membranes form, why proteins fold, and why DNA strands pair. Polarity is the single most important concept connecting chemistry to biology. You will never escape electronegativity. It comes back in organic chemistry, biochemistry, and physiology. The Pauling scale values are the same everywhere.

03Hydrogen Bonding

Now that you understand polarity (Section 02) and lone pairs (Section 01), you have everything you need to understand hydrogen bonding, which is the single most important intermolecular force in biology.

What Is a Hydrogen Bond?

A hydrogen bond is an attraction between two molecules (or two parts of the same large molecule). Specifically, it is the attraction between a δ+ hydrogen on one molecule and a lone pair of electrons on an electronegative atom (O, N, or F) of another molecule. The hydrogen must be covalently bonded to an electronegative atom (making it δ+), and the atom it is attracted to must have a lone pair available (providing the δ− electron density for the attraction). Both ingredients are required: a partially positive hydrogen and a lone pair on an electronegative neighbor.

How It Works in Water

In liquid water, the δ+ hydrogen of one water molecule is attracted to a lone pair on the δ− oxygen of a neighboring water molecule. This is the hydrogen bond. Now here is where the count of four comes from. Each water molecule has two hydrogens, each of which can form a hydrogen bond with a neighbor's lone pair. Each water molecule also has two lone pairs on its oxygen, each of which can accept a hydrogen bond from a neighbor's hydrogen. Two donated plus two accepted gives a maximum of four hydrogen bonds per water molecule.

Why Lone Pairs Matter Here

Without lone pairs, there would be no hydrogen bonding. The lone pair is the electron-rich site that attracts the electron-poor hydrogen. Think of it this way: the hydrogen is δ+ because its electrons were pulled away by oxygen. It is essentially a partially exposed proton. The lone pair on the neighboring oxygen is a concentrated pocket of negative charge. Opposite charges attract. That attraction, across the gap between two separate molecules, is the hydrogen bond.

Key Point
Individual hydrogen bonds are weak, roughly 5% the strength of a covalent bond. But water molecules form vast networks of hydrogen bonds simultaneously. The collective effect of millions of weak bonds explains every unusual physical property of water: high boiling point, high specific heat, high heat of vaporization, surface tension, and ice floating.

To put hydrogen bonds in perspective, here is how they compare to other types of attractions. The strength values below are approximate averages measured experimentally by determining how much energy it takes to break each type of bond (in kilojoules per mole of bonds broken).

Bond TypeTypical StrengthWhere It ActsExample

Common Mistake
A hydrogen bond is NOT a covalent bond. Despite having "bond" in its name, it is an intermolecular attraction (between separate molecules), not an intramolecular bond (within a single molecule). The covalent O-H bonds hold the atoms together inside one water molecule. Hydrogen bonds hold separate water molecules to each other. The covalent bond is about 20 times stronger than a hydrogen bond. If you break a covalent bond, you destroy the molecule. If you break a hydrogen bond, you just separate two molecules that were attracted to each other. Another common error: students say the hydrogen bond forms "between two hydrogens" or "between two oxygens." Neither is correct. The hydrogen bond forms between one molecule's hydrogen (δ+) and another molecule's oxygen lone pair (δ−).
Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
Hydrogen bonds show up everywhere in biology, and in every case the mechanism is the same: a δ+ hydrogen attracted to a lone pair on an electronegative atom. In DNA, the bases on opposite strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between N-H groups (hydrogen donors) and lone pairs on oxygen or nitrogen atoms (hydrogen acceptors). Adenine pairs with thymine through 2 hydrogen bonds; guanine pairs with cytosine through 3 hydrogen bonds. In proteins, hydrogen bonds between the N-H of one peptide bond and the lone pair on the C=O oxygen of another peptide bond create the alpha helix and beta sheet secondary structures. In plants, hydrogen bonds between water molecules allow water to be pulled through xylem vessels from roots to leaves. The intermolecular force is identical in every case. If you understand it in water, you understand it everywhere.

04Cohesion, Adhesion & Surface Tension

Cohesion is the attraction of water molecules to other water molecules. This is a direct consequence of hydrogen bonding. Because water molecules are constantly forming hydrogen bonds with their neighbors, they tend to stick together.

Adhesion is the attraction of water molecules to other polar surfaces, such as the walls of a glass tube or the cellulose in plant cell walls. Adhesion also depends on polarity and hydrogen bonding, just directed at a surface instead of at another water molecule.

Surface tension is the result of cohesion at the air-water interface. Water molecules at the surface have no water neighbors above them, so they form hydrogen bonds only with the molecules beside and below them. This creates a net inward pull that makes the surface behave like a stretched elastic film. Insects walk on water because of surface tension.

Capillary action is the combined result of cohesion and adhesion. When a narrow tube is placed in water, adhesion pulls water molecules up the walls of the tube, and cohesion pulls the rest of the water column along with them. The narrower the tube, the higher the water rises.

Real-World Example

In a plant, water travels from roots to leaves through xylem vessels, which are very narrow tubes. Adhesion holds water to the xylem walls. Cohesion keeps the water column intact as transpiration (evaporation from leaves) pulls water upward. This is the cohesion-tension theory of water transport, and it works in trees over 100 meters tall without any pump.

Key Point
A meniscus in a graduated cylinder is caused by adhesion (water climbing the glass walls) and cohesion (the rest of the water being pulled along). You read the volume at the bottom of the meniscus because that is the true level of the bulk water.

05Thermal Properties

Water has three thermal properties that are unusually high compared to similar-sized molecules, and all three are explained by hydrogen bonding.

High Specific Heat

Specific heat is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius. Water's specific heat is 4.18 J/g·°C, which is much higher than most substances. The reason: when you add heat to water, much of that energy goes into breaking hydrogen bonds rather than increasing the speed of the molecules (which is what temperature measures). So water resists temperature change.

Heat Equation
q = mcΔT
q = heat (J), m = mass (g), c = specific heat (J/g·°C), ΔT = change in temperature

High Heat of Vaporization

The heat of vaporization is the energy required to convert 1 gram of liquid to gas at its boiling point. For water, this is 2,260 J/g (or 40.7 kJ/mol). This is extremely high because to evaporate, a water molecule must break all of its hydrogen bonds to escape into the gas phase. Evaporative cooling (sweating, transpiration) works because the molecules that leave carry away a large amount of heat energy, cooling the surface they leave behind.

High Boiling Point

Based on molecular weight alone, water (MW 18) should be a gas at room temperature. Methane (MW 16) boils at −161°C. But water boils at 100°C because hydrogen bonds hold the molecules together much more tightly than the weak van der Waals forces between methane molecules.

Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
The heat equation q = mcΔT appears in general chemistry (calorimetry, thermochemistry), physics (thermodynamics), and biology (body temperature regulation, ecology of aquatic environments). The concept is identical. Water's high specific heat is why coastal cities have milder climates than inland cities, why your body temperature stays stable, and why aquatic organisms are buffered against rapid temperature swings.

06Water as a Solvent

Water is called the "universal solvent" not because it dissolves everything, but because it dissolves more different substances than any other common liquid. The reason is polarity. Water's partial charges allow it to interact with and pull apart ionic compounds and other polar molecules.

How Water Dissolves Ionic Compounds

When NaCl is placed in water, the δ− oxygen of water molecules is attracted to the Na+ ions, and the δ+ hydrogens are attracted to the Cl− ions. Water molecules surround each ion individually, a process called hydration (or solvation). The hydration shell of water molecules stabilizes the separated ions in solution and prevents them from recombining.

Hydrophilic vs. Hydrophobic

Hydrophilic ("water-loving") substances are polar or ionic and dissolve easily in water. Examples: salts, sugars, amino acids with charged side chains. Hydrophobic ("water-fearing") substances are nonpolar and do not dissolve in water. Examples: fats, oils, hydrocarbon chains. The rule is "like dissolves like": polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.

Amphipathic (or amphiphilic) molecules have both a hydrophilic region and a hydrophobic region. Phospholipids are the classic example: their polar head group faces water, and their nonpolar fatty acid tails face away from water. This dual nature is what drives the spontaneous formation of cell membranes.

Key Point
The entire structure of cell membranes exists because of water. Phospholipids arrange themselves into bilayers specifically to satisfy the thermodynamic preference of hydrophobic tails to avoid water and hydrophilic heads to contact water. No water, no membranes, no cells.

07Ice & Density

Most substances are denser as solids than as liquids. Water is the famous exception. Ice is less dense than liquid water, which is why ice floats. The density of liquid water at 4°C is 1.00 g/mL. The density of ice at 0°C is about 0.917 g/mL.

The reason: in liquid water, hydrogen bonds are constantly forming, breaking, and reforming. Molecules are in motion and can pack closely together. When water freezes, the molecules lock into a rigid crystalline lattice where each molecule forms four hydrogen bonds in a fixed tetrahedral arrangement. This lattice has open spaces built into it, which means the solid takes up more volume than the same mass of liquid.

Key Point
Ice floating is biologically essential. If ice sank, lakes and oceans would freeze from the bottom up, killing aquatic organisms and preventing the insulating layer of surface ice that keeps deeper water liquid through winter. Life in aquatic environments depends on this anomalous property.

Water reaches its maximum density at 4°C, not at 0°C. This means the densest, heaviest water sinks to the bottom of a lake at 4°C, and colder water (approaching 0°C) actually rises. This creates a temperature stratification that protects organisms living at the bottom of freshwater bodies.

Common Mistake
The question "why does ice float?" is not answered by saying "because it is less dense." That is circular. The real answer is that the hydrogen-bonded crystal lattice of ice has an open structure that occupies more volume per molecule than liquid water. The lattice geometry is the cause; lower density is the result.

08pH, Acids & Bases

Pure water undergoes a slight self-ionization: a tiny fraction of water molecules donate a proton to a neighbor, producing H3O+ (hydronium) and OH− (hydroxide). The concentrations of these ions in pure water at 25°C are both 1 × 10−7 M.

Ion Product of Water
Kw = [H+][OH−] = 1 × 10−14
at 25°C. This relationship always holds in any aqueous solution.
pH
pH = −log[H+]
pH 7 = neutral, pH < 7 = acidic, pH > 7 = basic

The pH scale is logarithmic. Each whole number change in pH represents a 10-fold change in hydrogen ion concentration. A solution at pH 3 has 10 times more H+ than pH 4 and 100 times more than pH 5.

Acids and Bases

DefinitionAcidBase
ArrheniusProduces H+ in waterProduces OH− in water
Brønsted-LowryProton (H+) donorProton (H+) acceptor

A strong acid (like HCl) dissociates completely in water. A weak acid (like acetic acid, CH3COOH) only partially dissociates, establishing an equilibrium between the intact acid and its ions. Most biologically relevant acids and bases are weak.

Worked Example

If [H+] = 1 × 10−4 M, what is the pH?

pH = −log(1 × 10−4) = 4

What is [OH−]?

[OH−] = Kw / [H+] = 10−14 / 10−4 = 10−10 M

Common Mistake
Lower pH means MORE acidic (more H+), not less. The scale runs in the opposite direction of concentration. pH 2 is strongly acidic; pH 12 is strongly basic. Students frequently get this backward.

09Buffers

A buffer is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. Buffers work because they contain both a weak acid (which can neutralize added base by donating H+) and its conjugate base (which can neutralize added acid by accepting H+).

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation
pH = pKa + log([A−]/[HA])
HA = weak acid, A− = conjugate base, pKa = −log(Ka)

A buffer works best when the pH of the solution is within about 1 pH unit of the pKa of the weak acid. At pKa, [HA] = [A−], meaning the buffer has equal capacity to neutralize added acid or base.

Biological Example

The bicarbonate buffer system is the primary pH buffer in human blood:

CO2 + H2O ↔ H2CO3 ↔ H+ + HCO3−

Normal blood pH is 7.35 to 7.45. If blood pH drops below 7.35, the condition is acidosis. Above 7.45 is alkalosis. Both are dangerous. The bicarbonate system, along with the lungs (which regulate CO2) and kidneys (which regulate HCO3−), maintains blood pH within this narrow range.

Cross-Disciplinary Bridge
Buffers connect general chemistry (equilibrium, weak acid/base chemistry, Henderson-Hasselbalch equation) directly to physiology (blood pH regulation), biochemistry (enzyme function requires specific pH ranges), and clinical medicine (metabolic acidosis/alkalosis, respiratory compensation). The chemistry is identical; only the context changes.

10Water in Living Systems

Body Water

The human body is approximately 60% water by mass. Water serves as the solvent for virtually all biochemical reactions, the medium for transport (blood plasma, lymph, cytoplasm), a reactant in hydrolysis and photosynthesis, a product in condensation reactions and cellular respiration, and a temperature regulator through evaporative cooling.

Hydrolysis & Dehydration Synthesis

Two of the most fundamental reactions in biology involve water directly. Dehydration synthesis (condensation) builds polymers by removing water: two monomers are joined and a water molecule is released. Hydrolysis breaks polymers apart by adding water: a water molecule is consumed to break the bond between monomers. Every time your body digests food, it is performing hydrolysis. Every time a cell builds a protein, it performs dehydration synthesis.

Photosynthesis & Cellular Respiration

Water is a reactant in photosynthesis (it is split to provide electrons and release O2) and a product of cellular respiration (formed at the end of the electron transport chain).

Photosynthesis (simplified)
6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2
Cellular Respiration (simplified)
C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + ATP
Key Point
Water is not just the background environment for life. It is an active participant in the chemistry of life, serving as a reactant, product, solvent, temperature buffer, and structural element (turgor pressure in plants). Understanding water is understanding the medium in which all biology occurs.
